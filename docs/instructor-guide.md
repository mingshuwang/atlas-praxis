# Atlas Praxis Instructor Guide

This guide shows how to use Atlas Praxis in a 30-60 minute class on visual reasoning, map use, and cartographic critique. The activity is designed for a computer lab, seminar room, or bring-your-own-device session. It does not require accounts, uploads, a backend, or external map services.

## Teaching Focus

Use Atlas Praxis when students need to practise:

- Explaining how classification changes map interpretation.
- Evaluating colour, contrast, labels, and visual hierarchy.
- Critiquing maps from the perspective of a map user.
- Moving from exploratory map design to a concise featured graphic.
- Discussing representation, perception, interpretation, ethics, and communication.

## Preparation

Before class:

1. Open the site and confirm the Studio page loads.
2. Decide whether students will work individually, in pairs, or in small groups.
3. Choose one mapped variable to start with, such as Transit accessibility or Green space access.
4. Ask students to keep notes on one design change, one interpretation change, and one critique point.

No course assessment details or internal marking criteria are needed for this exercise.

## 30-Minute Class Plan

1. Orientation, 5 minutes  
   Introduce the learning pathway: Observe -> Modify -> Critique -> Communicate. Emphasise that the goal is to reason with map design, not to find a single correct map.

2. Studio exploration, 10 minutes  
   Students open Studio and change one setting at a time: classification, colour palette, number of classes, opacity, or labels. They record what becomes more visible and what becomes less visible.

3. Critique framework, 10 minutes  
   Students use the Critique page to discuss Representation, Perception, Interpretation, Ethics, and Communication. Each group should identify one strength and one design risk.

4. Debrief, 5 minutes  
   Ask students to state one geographic claim the map supports and one condition under which that claim would need qualification.

## 60-Minute Class Plan

1. Orientation, 5 minutes  
   Frame Atlas Praxis as a studio for applied map design and visual reasoning.

2. Observe, 10 minutes  
   Students inspect the default choropleth and describe the apparent spatial pattern before changing any controls.

3. Modify, 15 minutes  
   Students test classification and colour choices. They compare equal interval and quantile classifications and note how the perceived pattern changes.

4. Critique, 15 minutes  
   Students apply the five-part framework. Encourage them to separate data representation choices from visual perception choices.

5. Communicate, 10 minutes  
   Students open Featured Graphics and turn their strongest interpretation into a working title, one annotation idea, and one takeaway sentence.

6. Share, 5 minutes  
   Each group gives a brief verbal account of the design choice that most changed their interpretation.

## Activity Prompts

Classification and interpretation:

- Which districts change class when you switch classification method?
- Does the map invite a different claim after the change?
- Which classification is easier to defend for this teaching question?

Colour and perception:

- Which palette best preserves ordered differences?
- Where does contrast guide attention first?
- Does opacity make the pattern easier to read or less decisive?

User-centred map critique:

- What would a non-specialist reader infer first?
- What information might they assume is more certain than it is?
- What single change would improve responsible interpretation?

From map to featured graphic:

- What is the geographic claim?
- What title would make that claim explicit?
- What annotation would help a reader understand the most important exception?

## Adaptation

Instructors can adapt the public copy, bundled data, or activity prompts for other geomatics topics as long as the app remains static. Suggested extensions include a second demonstration dataset, a side-by-side comparison mode, or a printable worksheet generated from the existing critique framework.
